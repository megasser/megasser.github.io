The Minerva Research Initiative

  • 9/11, the War on Terror as the anthropologists' war
  • "Supporting social science for a safer world"
  • "a DoD-sponsored, university-based social science research initiative that focuses on areas of strategic importance to the U.S. national security policy"
  • Programs
    • University research grants: 70 3+-year grants to date
    • Iinvesting in the defense experts who teach our future military and national security leaders at professional military education (PME) institutions and military service academies
    • Collaborating with the US Institute of Peace to offer dissertation fellowships and early career faculty awards
  • In first year funding split between DoD and NSF; later only DoD
  • Improve relations between DoD and universities
  • Annual fall conference since 2011
  • Outsourcing of work to universities

Reponse of anthropologists and other social scientists (pre-Minerva)

  • Opposition
    • The AAA position (Statement on Ethics, 1967; Principles of Professional Practice, 1971); "secret research", "secret reports, debriefings"
    • Opposition: threatened confidence from communities studied, information used to control or annihilate communities
    • Network of Concerned Anthropologists (2007-), The Counter-Counterinsurgency Manual (2009)
    • "scholars are wary of support from an external source with an interest in applying the results" (Engermann, 2009)
    • Not applied social scientists but functioning as "supplying information to a powerful client" (Horowitz, 1967)
  • Support
    • (Official) "it is far more effective and economical to avoid insurgency through essentially constructive efforts than to counter it after it has grown into a full-scale movement requiring drastically greater effort."
    • The "bad anthropology" argument (Anna Simons, 2002, NPR)

The Minerva Research Initiative: responses

  • Ethical concerns: how Iraqi documents "came into the possession" DoD
  • Selection biases, lack of DoD infrostructure for peer review; skewing in who applies
  • Gunderson: "Pentagon’s long term goal is to develop a cadre of social scientists, particularly in anthropology, who are tied to the military and its projects"
  • AAA president: "we are concerned that the Department of Defense would turn for assistance in developing a selection process to those who are not intimately familiar with the rigorous standards of our discipline."
  • Undercuts critical thinking: "the military definition of reality. This entails seeing the world as a series of threats to be dealt with, sorting people into enemies and allies, and focusing on the use or threat of force".
  • What about climate change, inequality, job loss, disease, nuclear proliferation, massive migration, food shortages, problems generated by the Pentagon itself?
  • How is "terrorism" defined?
  • Gusterson: "It is my most profound hope that anthropologists will refuse to transform their discipline into one that uses a rhetorical patina of cross-cultural understanding and harm reduction to mask a project that would understand the other in order to subjugate and control it. This would be a betrayal of our human subjects and of our vocation as interlocutors of the other."
  • How can anthropologists challenge (or study those challenging) the State when they're working for it?
  • Lutz (2008): "To join this project is to lend scholarship’s good name to a similarly grandiose, dangerous, and irrelevant project of building a better way to make war and maintain US military dominance of the globe."
